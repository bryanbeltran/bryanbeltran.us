This was the first year I tried to start a garden from seed in a serious way. I garden in Richfield, Minnesota — I treat it as USDA zone **5a**, with a **mid-May** last frost. [SeedStarter](/projects) was not ready in time for this spring, so the calendar lived in my head. Next season, the tool gets a real turn.

As of midsummer, **everything that made it into the ground is still alive.** The tomatoes are growing hard and setting a lot of fruit — Norfolk Healthy Foods purple, Purple Cherokee, and beefsteak all look like the seed-start gamble paid off.

## Why start from seed

I wanted varieties I could not reliably find as transplants:

- Little Gem lettuce
- Norfolk Healthy Foods purple tomato
- Purple Cherokee
- Beefsteak
- Purple tomatillo
- Serrano chiles
- Asparagus (established bed plus a new patch)

Store shelves are fine for generic plants. They are less fine when you care about a specific purple tomato or a tomatillo that is not whatever the big box carried that week.

I am also forgetful. A good plan beats vibes when every crop has a different indoor-start offset from last frost.

## What I actually did

Almost everything started indoors. The exception was lettuce — I direct-sowed a lot of Little Gem. That part worked, until it worked too well: we could not eat through it before it bolted. The hens were happy about that outcome. I was less happy about the timing, but wasted lettuce that feeds animals is not a total loss.

Tomatoes and serranos were the crops I cared about getting right from seed. Midsummer update: the tomato plants are full of set fruit. Tomatillos were the expensive lesson before transplant.

## The tomatillo failure

I started purple tomatillos too early. They got leggy on the windowsill, then they died — they never made it to the garden. Classic cold-climate indoor-start failure: too many weeks before transplant, not enough light, plants that look busy and then collapse.

That is exactly the failure mode [I wrote about](/blog/usda-zone-frost-date-planting) in the frost-date post — and the reason SeedStarter exists. Mid-May last frost only helps if you count backward correctly per crop. "Start early so they are big" is how you get weak stems.

## Asparagus is different

I have an established asparagus bed and started a new patch this year. I would not start asparagus indoors again. Scattering seed in winter has been more effective for me than trying to nurse starts inside. Perennials do not follow the same tomato playbook.

## What SeedStarter needs to own next spring

The tool should be strong at **peppers and tomatoes** first — indoor start dates, transplant after mid-May, no more guessing on tomatillos and chiles.

After that I want **succession planting** for lettuce and similar crops: not one giant sowing that bolts into chicken feed, but staggered plantings so harvest stays ahead of the plant.

If you are in the Twin Cities metro and this is also your first real seed-start year, start with last frost, write offsets down (or let software do it), and do not "gift" tomatillos an extra month indoors. I already ran that experiment.
